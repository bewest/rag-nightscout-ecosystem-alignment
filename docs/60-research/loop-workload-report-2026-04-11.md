# Loop Workload as Settings Adequacy Metric

**Date**: 2026-04-11  
**Experiments**: EXP-2391 through EXP-2396  
**Patients**: 19 (11 Nightscout + 8 ODC)  

## Executive Summary

We investigated whether loop workload (how much the AID loop deviates from
scheduled basal) can serve as a settings adequacy metric. The results reveal a
more fundamental finding:

**AID loops never operate at scheduled rates.** 18/19 patients show maximally
high loop modulation (workload score saturates at 100/100). The only exception
is patient j, whose loop barely modifies the scheduled basal.

This means the "scheduled basal rate" is a fiction for AID users — the loop
rewrites it constantly. The implication is that **settings adequacy cannot be
assessed from TIR** (r = -0.165, no correlation) and instead must be inferred
from the magnitude of loop deviation.

## Key Findings

### EXP-2391: Universal High Workload

| Direction | Count | Meaning |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| REDUCING (basal suspension dominant) | 14 | Over-basaled — loop constantly suspends |
| INCREASING (basal increase dominant) | 4 | Under-basaled — loop constantly increases |
| NEUTRAL | 1 | Settings adequate (patient j only) |

- Mean basal suspension rate: 60% (range 0–99%)
- Mean basal increase rate: 10% (range 0–54%)
- **Patient j is the only patient where the loop operates at scheduled rate**

### EXP-2392: Workload vs TIR (r = -0.165)

**No correlation between loop workload and TIR.** This is the definitive
validation of the AID Compensation Theorem: the loop achieves similar TIR
regardless of how hard it works. Settings quality does not predict outcomes.

### EXP-2393: Workload vs Hypo Risk (r = 0.238)

Weak positive correlation — more loop work is weakly associated with more hypo
events. This makes mechanistic sense: aggressive basal suspension creates IOB
uncertainty, and when the loop misjudges, hypoglycemia results.

### EXP-2394: Workload by Period

Workload is uniformly high across all periods (overnight, morning, afternoon,
evening). There is no "clean" period where the loop relaxes. This extends the
overnight finding (EXP-2373) to 24 hours.

### EXP-2395: Workload Stability

Workload is stable over time for most patients — settings don't change, so
the loop maintains its constant compensation pattern. Two ODC patients show
worsening workload, suggesting progressive settings drift.

### EXP-2396: Settings Adequacy Scores

Using workload-based scoring: 17/19 patients receive grade D, 1 receives F,
and only patient j receives B. **No patient achieves grade A.** This
reflects the universal AID compensation pattern.

## Discussion

### The "Scheduled Basal" Misnomer

For AID patients, the scheduled basal rate is not the basal rate the patient
receives. The loop modifies it 60-100% of the time. This has implications:

1. **Endocrinology consultations reviewing "basal rates" are reviewing fiction** —
   the actual basal delivered bears little resemblance to what's configured
2. **Autotune and similar tools that adjust scheduled rates are solving the wrong
   problem** — the loop will just re-compensate
3. **The true metric is the loop's workload** — how much effort does the loop
   expend to maintain glycemia?

### Why the Workload Score Saturates

The 0-100 score saturates because the normalization threshold (0.5 mean deviation)
is too low — almost all patients exceed it. This reflects a genuine finding:
the magnitude of loop deviation is uniformly extreme. Future work should use
percentile-based normalization or raw deviation metrics.

### Practical Recommendation

The most actionable metric from this analysis is **net direction**:
- **REDUCING patients (14/19)**: scheduled basal is too high, loop compensates
  by suspending. Recommendation: decrease scheduled basal.
- **INCREASING patients (4/19)**: scheduled basal is too low, loop compensates
  by increasing. Recommendation: increase scheduled basal.
- **NEUTRAL (1/19)**: settings are well-calibrated. No changes needed.
